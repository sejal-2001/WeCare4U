Kidney Disease

Overview

Chronic kidney disease, also known as chronic kidney failure, refers to the gradual decline in kidney function. The kidneys play a crucial role in filtering waste and excess fluids from the blood, which are then eliminated through urine. As chronic kidney disease progresses to advanced stages, dangerous levels of fluids, electrolytes, and wastes can accumulate in the body. In the early stages, there may be minimal signs or symptoms, and chronic kidney disease may only become apparent when kidney function is significantly impaired. Treatment for chronic kidney disease focuses on slowing down the progression of kidney damage, typically by addressing the underlying cause. Without artificial filtering (dialysis) or a kidney transplant, chronic kidney disease can progress to end-stage kidney failure, which is life-threatening.

Symptoms

Signs and symptoms of chronic kidney disease are experienced gradually over time as kidney damage progresses. These may include:

  • Nausea and Vomiting
  • Loss of appetite
  • Fatigue , weakness and sleep problems
  • Changes in how much you urinate
  • High blood pressure (hypertension) that's difficult to control
  • Decreased mental sharpness
  • Muscle twitches and cramps
  • Shortness of breath, if fluid builds up in the lungs
  • Chest pain, if fluid builds up around the lining of the heart
