Stroke

Overview

A stroke occurs when the flow of blood to a certain part of the brain is disrupted or diminished, leading to a deprivation of oxygen and essential nutrients to the brain tissue. This interruption results in the rapid death of brain cells within a matter of minutes. Timely and immediate medical intervention is of utmost importance since a stroke is considered a medical emergency.

Taking early action can significantly minimize brain damage and potential complications. Fortunately, the number of stroke-related fatalities in the United States has significantly decreased over time. The availability of effective treatments has also contributed to preventing disabilities resulting from strokes.

Symptoms

In a situation where there is a chance of experiencing a stroke, It's really important to remember and make a note of when the symptoms of a stroke started. Getting certain treatments quickly after a stroke begins can have the best results. Knowing and recognizing the signs and symptoms of a stroke is vital, and they can include:

  • Trouble speaking and understanding what others are saying. You may experience confusion, slur your words or have difficulty understanding speech.
  • Paralysis or numbness of the face, arm or leg. You may develop sudden numbness, weakness or paralysis in your face, arm or leg.
  • Problems seeing in one or both eyes. You may suddenly have blurred or blackened vision in one or both eyes, or you may see double.
  • Headache. A sudden, severe headache, which may be accompanied by vomiting, dizziness or altered consciousness, may indicate that you're having a stroke.
  • Trouble walking. You may stumble or lose your balance. You may also have sudden dizziness or a loss of coordination.
